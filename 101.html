Disinformation Mirror

Duration: 10 weeks
Type: Group project
Context: Master Strategic Product Design · TU Delft
Client: Belastingdienst / FIOD
Focus: Systemic design · AI · Disinformation · Critical thinking

The aim of this project was to explore how disinformation, AI-generated content and online manipulation affect society’s ability to recognise and respond to financial crime. Together with the FIOD, we looked beyond disinformation as a single fake message, and approached it as a systemic challenge shaped by hyperdigitalisation, echo chambers, declining trust and reduced critical thinking.

Social network analysis showing the actors involved in disinformation, financial crime and digital safety.
The challenge

The challenge

As AI-generated content, deepfakes and online scams become increasingly realistic, it becomes harder for people to distinguish what is real from what is manipulated. This does not only affect individuals, but also creates wider societal consequences: distrust, polarisation and increased vulnerability to financial crime. Our challenge was to explore how citizens could become more critically aware of the digital content they encounter.

System map visualising how AI, hyperdigitalisation, trust, polarisation and critical thinking reinforce each other.
Our approach

Our approach

We used stakeholder mapping, system mapping, future speculation and interviews with scam victims to understand where an intervention could create meaningful impact. A key insight was that the problem was not only about detecting fake content, but about creating a moment of friction: a pause in which people start questioning what they see online.

Future cone exploring possible, probable and preferred futures in relation to AI, automation and digital trust.
The concept

The concept

Our final concept, The Disinformation Mirror, is a speculative photo booth experience. At first, it feels like a simple public photo booth. But instead of receiving a normal photo, participants are confronted with a fake AI-generated news item or video involving themselves. This personal and emotional moment makes the abstract threat of disinformation tangible.

The intervention consists of a provocative photo booth, an educational webpage and a recurring Scam of the Month challenge.
How it works

How it works

After the confrontation, participants are directed to a webpage with tools to recognise manipulation, deepfakes and scams. A recurring Scam of the Month challenge helps people keep practising their critical thinking, while also generating insights into public vulnerability to different types of scams.

Step-by-step user journey of the Disinformation Mirror experience.
My role

My role

My role focused on concept development, system mapping and stakeholder research. I helped structure the complexity of the problem, connect insights across the different system maps, translate stakeholder and interview findings into design opportunities, and develop the final speculative intervention. I was especially involved in shaping the idea of using a personal, confronting experience to make the abstract threat of AI-generated disinformation tangible.

Drawing Human Figures

Duration: 10 weeks
Year: 2025
Type: Drawing studies
Focus: Human anatomy · Proportions · Movement · Gesture

A collection of drawing studies focused on understanding the human figure. During this 10-week process, I practised proportions, body structure, poses, hands, heads and movement through quick sketches, construction exercises and reference-based studies.

Figure construction

Figure construction

These first studies explore how the body can be built up from simple shapes, helping me understand posture, balance and proportions.

Early construction studies, using simple forms to understand the structure of the human figure.
Figure studies exploring standing, leaning and seated poses.
Gesture and movement

Gesture and movement

I practised different body positions to better understand movement, weight distribution and dynamic poses.

Quick gesture studies with different poses and directions of movement.
Studies focused on bending, reaching and shifting body weight.
Body variation

Body variation

These studies helped me practise different body types and proportions, moving away from one fixed idea of the human figure.

Studies exploring variation in body proportions and silhouette.
Reference-based figure studies with different poses and body shapes.
Details

Details

Alongside full-body studies, I also practised more detailed elements such as heads, facial construction and hands.

Head construction studies, practising angles, planes and facial structure.
Hand studies focused on proportion, gesture and finger construction.
Application

Application

The final studies apply the figure drawing practice to more complete scenes, combining posture, movement, clothing and reference-based observation.

Applied studies combining human figures with movement, clothing and simple scene context.
Additional figure studies focused on pose, proportion and the direction of body lines, using reference images to better understand movement and construction.
